The Long Way In

A 20-image photographic narrative tracing the full trajectory of African footballers — from the hope and sacrifice of departure, through the racial hostility of arrival, to the psychological toll it exacts, and finally to the acts of resistance and refusal that refuse to let the story end there.

20 Images
4 Chapters
2024–2026
Ahmed Baba Tandia
I
Chapter One — Images 1–4

Between Dreams and Borders

The world before departure. Through still life compositions of everyday objects — boots, passports, clocks, family photographs — this chapter argues that for many young African players, football is not sport. It is a strategy of survival, a collective wager placed by entire families on the body and talent of one person.

The Journey Ball
A soccer ball, suitcase, and atlas map — the dream carried across borders.
Not Just a Game
Play versus survival — the jersey against the controller.
Identity on Pause
Passport, fabric, stopped clock — time suspended between worlds.
The Price of Dreams
Boots, money, family photo, schoolbook — who pays the hidden cost?
II
Chapter Two — Images 5–8

Upon Arrival

The dream lands — not in triumph, but in a system already prepared to use it. Drawing from the documented experiences of Vinícius Júnior, Mario Balotelli, Marcus Rashford, and Victor Osimhen, this chapter places the artist's own body inside the visual language of racial abuse, mapping how racism operates across the crowd, the media, the field, and the contract.

"OOH OOH" — Monkey Chants
Inspired by Vinícius Júnior — the psychological weight of racialized chants made visible.
"Why Always Me?"
Inspired by Mario Balotelli — the media wall, the television, one foot raised against the narrative.
Performance Pressure — After the Miss
Inspired by Rashford, Saka, Sancho — bird's-eye view, England jersey, face covered, grass below.
Media Mockery — Commodification
Inspired by Victor Osimhen — Nigerian jersey, a price tag held in the hand.
III
Chapter Three — Images 9–14

Impact

What the system produces in the body, mind, spirit, and emotions of a person who survives it. This chapter moves inward — from the visible hostility of the crowd and the media to the psychological emptiness, spiritual anchoring, enforced silence, physical objectification, and the blue quiet of aftermath.

The Empty Chair
Mental toll — the absent patient, the care never given.
Faith as Anchor
Quran, Bible, prayer beads — devotion that crosses borders.
Sealed
Tape across the mouth — eyes carry everything the mouth cannot say.
The Instrument
Skeleton. Barcode. No person. Only parts.
The Body
After the barcode — a spotlight, a field, a person. Fully present.
Blue Hour
A path curves out of frame — hope as direction, not destination.
IV
Chapter Four — Images 15–20

Resistance & Change

Knowledge, protest, solidarity, collective refusal, institutional accountability, and the final argument — beneath every visible difference, the substance is the same. This chapter does not resolve the story. It demands something of it.

Read the Room
Books hold the boots. Knowledge holds the game.
The Protest
The body kneels. The shadow stands — fist raised.
Hear No Evil, See No Evil, Speak No Evil
Three figures — collective refusal to absorb what the world keeps sending.
Why Always Us?
"Why always me?" / "Why always us?" / "Why never them?"
The Press Conference
The microphone. The placard reads "THEM." The chair is empty.
We Are All the Same
Two eggs. Two hands. One pan. One light. The most undeniable truth.

Biography

Ahmed Baba Tandia was born in January 2005 in Bamako, Mali, where he was raised until the age of thirteen, when he moved to the United States to join his siblings and continue his education. He is currently a rising senior at Whitman College in Walla Walla, Washington, pursuing a Bachelor's degree in Computer Science, with a graduation date set for May 2027.

Football has been a constant throughout his life — he played competitively through his senior year of high school, where he was offered a full scholarship to Whitman College. It is through the sport that his relationship to questions of race, belonging, and visibility first formed, long before they became the subject of his photography.

Tandia's relationship with photography began informally, during Eid celebrations in high school, where he was always the one behind the camera — playfully competing with his older brother over who could find the best light and the strongest frame. What started as a game became a serious practice when, in the spring semester of his junior year, he enrolled in Intermediate Photography at Whitman College under Professor Robin North.

It was during that course — researching notable photographers for a class presentation — that Tandia encountered the work of Omar Victor Diop. Diop's project Diaspora, in which he uses self-portraiture and football references to explore the duality of Black visibility and exclusion, gave Tandia a language for something he had long felt but had not yet known how to express. "I had always wanted to do something," he has said, "but I never knew how."

The result was The Long Way In — a 20-image photographic narrative structured in four chapters, tracing the full arc of the African footballer's journey: the hope and sacrifice before departure, the racial hostility of arrival in Western leagues, the psychological and physical toll exacted by that system, and the acts of resistance that refuse to let the story end there. What began as a class project is now the foundation of an ongoing social justice initiative, with ambitions to reach international audiences through exhibition, publication, and fellowship.

Tandia's practice draws on the traditions of conceptual still life photography and studio self-portraiture. His influences include Omar Victor Diop and the broader lineage of African photographers who use image-making as a form of testimony. He is based between Walla Walla, Washington and Auburn, Washington.
